# City of Toronto Policies 

## What is Municipal Policy? 

In the context of Toronto City Council, a policy is a Council-approved framework, principle, or set of guidelines that defines the City’s intent, priorities, and approach to an issue. 

Policies guide how decisions are made and how the City acts; they are not enforceable laws. City by-laws are passed by Council to regulate behaviour and activities, and are legally enforced by City staff (e.g., Municipal Licensing & Standards). 

Policies help the City:
- Set priorities (such as housing, transit, or climate action)
- Make consistent decisions across departments
- Guide City staff when designing programs or recommending actions
- Establish expectations for City agencies and corporations

## Who Creates Policy?

Policy-making is a shared process between elected City Councillors and City Staff. It is a collaborative effort: staff provide evidence and options, while councillors make final decisions.

**City Councillors**: As the elected representatives of Toronto’s residents, Councillors are responsible for setting the high-level priorities and values that guide the city’s future. They serve as the final decision-makers, debating and voting on proposed plans to officially adopt or amend policies into law.

**City Staff**: As the architects of the policy process, City staff research the issues and data needed to build policy options. They bridge the gap between the government and the public by consulting with residents, stakeholders, and experts to provide Council with evidence-based options and recommendations.

Much of this work takes place at the committee level, where smaller groups of councillors focus on specific policy areas (e.g., transportation, housing, or the environment) before matters are brought to full Council.

## How is Policy Developed? 

The policy development process is a 5-step lifecycle that transforms issues or the need for policy updates into measurable actions. By moving from data-driven research and engaging the public to formal approval and ongoing evaluation, the City ensures that new policies are both evidence-based and responsive to the community's needs. 

### 1. A Problem or Idea Is Identified
#### Goal: Clearly define the problem and what the policy outcomes would look like.

Policy creation/updates happen because:
- It is required by law 
  - E.g., When the Province passes a new Housing Bill, Toronto is legally required to update its zoning bylaws to match.
- City staff identifies a new issue or the need for an update  
  - E.g., City planners notice a sudden rise in e-scooters on sidewalks and realize new safety rules are needed to protect pedestrians.
- City Council or a committee makes a request 
  - E.g., A Councillor hears from parents about speeding in school zones and successfully moves a motion for the Transportation Committee to take action.
- City leadership sets a strategic priority
  - E.g., To hit climate targets, City leadership makes TransformTO Net Zero Strategy a top priority, leading to new policies that phase out gas-powered leaf blowers.

### 2. Research & Analysis 
#### Goal: Make sure decisions are evidence-based.

City staff:
- Reviews data and research
- Compares approaches of other large cities 
- Checks alignment with the City’s strategic plan and long-term goals
- Uses open data and economic dashboards about Toronto (population, neighbourhoods, needs) to inform policies
- E.g., jurisdictional scan, qualitative/quantitative analysis, etc. 


### 3. Draft Policy Options + Public Engagement 
#### This is the best time for you to get involved!

City staff:
- Creates multiple policy options before providing recommendations 
- Asks for input from other City Divisions, residents, community groups, businesses, and partners – this helps ensure policy design and outcomes reflect your interests 
- Have your say and share concerns/feedback (public consultations, surveys, advisory groups) 


### 4. Review & Approval
#### Goal: Final decisions and official approval.

The draft policy is reviewed by different actors depending on the policy, including:
- Subject-matter experts and key stakeholders 
- City Council committees, Division Directors/Heads, City Managers
- City Council 


### 5. Implement, Monitor, & Evaluate 
#### Goal: Turn direction into real-world action.

Once approved:
- Ongoing monitoring & evaluation to ensure policy outcomes are achieved 
- Performance measurement: How much did we do? How well did we do it? Is anyone better off?
